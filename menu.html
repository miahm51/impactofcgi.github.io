Early Development

The origins of computer-generated imagery (CGI) can be traced back to the 1970s and early 1980s, a period marked by advancements in digital imaging and computer graphics that laid the foundation for modern applications. At first, CGI was largely limited to simple wireframe models and low-resolution animations, restricted by the era's processing power and software technology (Das, 2023) However, several innovative projects showcased the potential of CGI. A notable example is the film Tron (1982), which was among the first to incorporate computer graphics into mainstream cinema, demonstrating how CGI could create new visual experiences that departed from traditional filmmaking techniques. The CGI used in Tron was groundbreaking, even though it was still in its early stages, as it revealed the possibilities of crafting computer-generated environments and visuals that exceeded the capabilities of practical effects.

The release of Jurassic Park in 1993 represented a critical moment in the evolution of CGI within the film industry. Steven Spielberg's innovative application of CGI to render realistic dinosaurs transformed filmmaking, demonstrating that CGI could serve not merely as a gimmick but as an essential narrative device that enhances realism and engages viewers in unprecedented manners. Jurassic Park established a benchmark for the realistic simulation of living creatures and environments, offering a model for numerous films and sectors to emulate (Pearson, 2023).

Technological Milestones

In the following decades, CGI technology experienced significant advancements, marked by innovations that greatly enhanced its realism, versatility, and user-friendliness. Notable achievements included the creation of 3D modeling and rendering software, which empowered creators to construct intricate digital models and replicate realistic textures, lighting, and shading. Programs such as Autodesk 3ds Max and Maya provided artists with the tools to produce detailed, lifelike visuals that could compete with the quality of traditional film (Amba, 2023).

Motion capture is a game-changing technology that has enabled CGI to accurately portray the details of human movement and emotions. This innovation has played a crucial role in crafting lifelike and expressive CGI characters, exemplified by Gollum in The Lord of the Rings trilogy. Andy Serkis's performance brought Gollum to life, showcasing how far CGI can go in expressing complex feelings. As technology continues to evolve, CGI gets even better at replicating real life, helping creators build immersive worlds and characters that seem genuine and believable (Adobe, 2024).

Modern-Day CGI

CGI has become a crucial part of visual media today, deeply integrated into movies, television, video games, and advertising. What used to be groundbreaking has transformed into a vital resource that opens endless creative opportunities. With the latest developments in artificial intelligence and machine learning, CGI has improved even more, making it easier for artists to create incredibly realistic images in a shorter amount of time. Now, CGI can also work alongside augmented and virtual reality, producing engaging experiences that mix the digital and real worlds (Das, 2023).

In today's media, CGI has become so advanced that viewers might not even notice they're seeing computer-generated images. Whether it's complete landscapes or actors who look younger thanks to digital effects, CGI has achieved such a realistic quality that it can make it hard to tell what's real and what's not. The widespread use of CGI has changed what audiences expect in terms of visual quality and has also expanded the creative possibilities for filmmakers, game developers, and advertisers, allowing them to realize their most daring concepts without being limited by the physical world (Das, 2023)

Transformation in Media Formats

The introduction of CGI has profoundly transformed not just individual films and video games but has also redefined entire media formats and the ways in which audiences interact with them. In the beginning, CGI was mainly utilized in blockbuster films due to its high costs and technological requirements, which limited its broader application. However, as technology progressed, CGI began to permeate other media formats, including television, video games, and anime, significantly expanding the reach and influence of computer-generated content across various platforms (Adobe, 2024).

CGI has revolutionized television by enabling the production of stunning visuals even with limited time and money. This change opened the door for fantasy and sci-fi shows like Game of Thrones to vividly portray intricate imaginary worlds that would have been unfeasible with traditional TV budgets (Morrison, 2021). Consequently, we are now experiencing a golden age of visually impressive television that competes with high-budget movies, altering what viewers expect in terms of visual quality across various media (Adobe, 2024).

Anime, which is usually recognized for its hand-drawn style, has started to use CGI to enhance its visual range and make production easier. In the past, CGI in anime often looked limited and stood out in a way that some viewers found distracting. However, with recent improvements in CGI technology, anime studios have been able to mix CGI with traditional animation more effectively, resulting in exciting scenes that would be tough to create by hand. Series like Land of the Lustrous and Beastars showcase how CGI can be used to create smooth, detailed animations and complex camera movements that add a unique touch to storytelling.
